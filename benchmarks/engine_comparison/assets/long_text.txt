The history of computing stretches back far longer than the silicon chips that
define the modern era. Long before electricity, human beings devised mechanical
aids to calculation. The abacus, used across the ancient world, allowed
merchants to add and subtract large sums with practiced speed. In the
seventeenth century, Blaise Pascal built a geared adding machine to ease his
father's tax-collecting work, and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz extended the idea
with a device that could multiply and divide. Leibniz also articulated the
binary system that would, three centuries later, become the native language of
every digital computer. These early machines were limited and fragile, but they
embodied a powerful conviction: that reasoning about numbers could be reduced to
the disciplined manipulation of physical parts.

The nineteenth century gave the field its most prophetic figure in Charles
Babbage. Frustrated by the errors that riddled the printed mathematical tables
of his day, Babbage designed the Difference Engine, a steam-powered calculator
meant to compute and typeset polynomial values without human mistakes. He never
finished it, but his ambition only grew. The Analytical Engine, which he
sketched in increasing detail over decades, anticipated the architecture of the
modern computer: a "store" for holding numbers, a "mill" for performing
arithmetic, and a mechanism for reading instructions from punched cards borrowed
from the Jacquard loom. Ada Lovelace, translating an account of the machine,
added notes longer than the original article and described how it might be
programmed to compute Bernoulli numbers. She grasped, more clearly than Babbage
himself, that such a machine could manipulate not just numbers but any symbols
governed by rules, including music and language.

The conceptual leap from special-purpose calculators to general-purpose
computers required a theory of computation. In 1936, Alan Turing proposed an
abstract device, now called the Turing machine, that could read and write
symbols on an infinite tape according to a finite table of rules. Turing showed
that a single "universal" machine could simulate any other, and that some
problems could never be solved by any mechanical procedure at all. This was both
a foundation and a boundary: it defined what computation could be while marking
the limits of what it could achieve. During the Second World War, Turing applied
these ideas in practice at Bletchley Park, where the electromechanical bombes he
helped design accelerated the breaking of German Enigma ciphers and shortened
the conflict.

The war accelerated the move from mechanical and electromechanical machines to
fully electronic ones. ENIAC, completed in 1945, used some eighteen thousand
vacuum tubes to perform thousands of additions per second, an astonishing speed
for its time. But ENIAC was programmed by physically rewiring it, a process that
could take days. The decisive innovation, captured in John von Neumann's widely
circulated report, was to store a program in the same memory as the data it
operated on. The stored-program concept meant that changing a computer's
behavior was as simple as loading new instructions, transforming the machine
from a fixed appliance into a flexible instrument. Nearly every computer built
since has followed this von Neumann architecture.

The invention of the transistor at Bell Labs in 1947 began the long shrinking of
the computer. Transistors were smaller, cooler, more reliable, and far less
power-hungry than vacuum tubes. The integrated circuit, which placed many
transistors on a single sliver of silicon, compounded the effect. Gordon Moore
observed in 1965 that the number of components on a chip was doubling at a steady
pace, an empirical trend that became a self-fulfilling industrial roadmap. As
circuits grew denser, computers grew cheaper and more capable, migrating from
air-conditioned corporate basements to office desks and eventually to pockets.

Software evolved alongside the hardware. Early programmers wrote in raw machine
code, then in assembly languages that gave mnemonic names to instructions. The
arrival of high-level languages such as FORTRAN and COBOL let people express
algorithms in something closer to mathematics and English, and compilers
translated those expressions into the machine's own tongue. Operating systems
emerged to manage the growing complexity of shared machines, scheduling tasks
and mediating access to memory and devices. The development of time-sharing
allowed many users to interact with a single computer at once, making the
machine feel responsive and personal in a way that batch processing never could.

Networking knit individual computers into something larger than the sum of their
parts. The ARPANET, built in the late 1960s, demonstrated that packet switching
could route data reliably across unreliable links. The protocols that grew out
of that work, especially TCP/IP, became the common language of a global network.
The World Wide Web, layered on top of the internet in the early 1990s, gave
ordinary people a simple way to publish and retrieve documents, and the network
effect did the rest. Within a generation, computing shifted from a discipline
practiced by specialists to an ambient utility woven through daily life.

Seen as a whole, this history is a story of abstraction. Each generation hid the
messy details of the layer beneath it, so that programmers could think in larger
and larger units without worrying about the electrons. The machine learning
systems of today, trained on vast corpora and run across thousands of
processors, sit atop this towering stack of abstractions, the latest chapter in a
project that began with beads sliding along a wire.
